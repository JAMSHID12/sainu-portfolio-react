01 / The Real Problem

The platform hadn't changed since 2008. Everything else had.

When I first logged into EOT's live platform, I found what camp directors had been living with for years. The dashboard greeted them with a grey silhouette avatar and the text "Hi David." A single subscription card showed four rows of raw numbers. And the most visually prominent element on the entire screen - the largest, most styled UI component - was a yellow button reading "Buy More Staff Accounts."

The Administration page was a list of five plain blue hyperlinks. The Manage Courses screen showed course names in a text list with pencil and bin icons - no thumbnails, no enrolled count, no performance data. Statistics was a full-page list of course names with "Course Stats / Individual Stats" text links beside each one. The E-mail Staff page showed eight bullet-point links on a plain white background. Nothing was scannable. Nothing communicated urgency. Nothing answered a director's actual question.

"I log in to check if my staff are ready for opening day and I genuinely don't know where to look. I end up calling support."

- Camp Director · stakeholder discovery interview

This was a structural failure: the platform was organised around EOT's internal product categories, not around how a camp director thinks about running a camp. Every redesign decision flows from this single insight.

7 real UX failures documented from the live platform
  • Dashboard: "Hi David" + 4 data rows + yellow "Buy More Staff Accounts" as hero CTA. Right half entirely blank.
  • Administration: 5 blue hyperlinks on a white page. No grouping, no icons that communicate meaning, no visual hierarchy.
  • Manage Courses: Text list + pencil/bin only. No thumbnails, no status, no enrolled count visible without clicking.
  • Manage Custom Modules: Plain data table + a create-module form below it on the same page. Feature completely buried.
  • Manage Staff: Name + email + edit/delete only. No photos, no last login, no progress, no course enrollment visible.
  • Statistics: Full-page text list of course names. Every number hidden behind another click.
  • E-mail Staff: 8 plain bullet-point links. No composition UI, no message history, no feedback on send status.

02 / My Role & What I Owned

Solo designer. Every stage. This is the live product.

I owned this project from the first discovery session to the live platform that all EOT clients use today. Every research session, architectural decision, wireframe, component, and the case made for pulling the chatbot into V1 - all mine.

In the final hi-fi phase - with 100+ screens to produce across light and dark modes - I brought in one freelance UI designer to accelerate production. All design decisions, system architecture, and rationale documentation remained my sole responsibility throughout.

Critically, I stayed involved after handoff. I attended post-launch client meetings, heard the migration friction firsthand, and brought those insights back to make the case for the V1 chatbot. That decision produced the biggest measurable business outcome of the project: the support team reduction from 7 to 2.

Phase 1 · Discovery
Live platform audit + stakeholder interviews
Before writing a research question, I logged into the live platform and documented every screen, flow, and navigation path - identifying 7 structural UX failures. Conducted discovery sessions with PM Josh Gadad and Dev Manager David Price to understand business constraints, goals, and the competitive landscape.
Phase 2 · Research
Competitive analysis, personas, empathy mapping
SWOT analysis against 3 direct competitors. Key finding: EOT's custom module builder was a genuine differentiator none of the three competitors matched - but the old UX buried it 4 navigation levels deep. Built primary camp director persona (age 30-60) from interview synthesis.
Phase 3 · Architecture
Full IA rebuild + 6-module mind mapping
Rebuilt the entire IA from scratch around director mental models. Key discovery: Staff Management alone had 11 distinct action types. This directly shaped the "More Actions" dropdown pattern - keeping power-user capabilities accessible without overwhelming the primary view.
Phase 4 · Design
Wireframes, flows, design system, hi-fi
Wireframes for all 6 modules. 5 critical user flows with video walkthroughs. Full design system: WCAG-tested colour palette, Open Sans type scale, 40+ icon library, complete component library. Hi-fi screens in both light and dark mode. Freelance designer joined for production volume.
Phase 5 · Migration
Handoff + 6-month client migration + chatbot
Figma prototypes and dev documentation handed off. I personally attended post-launch client meetings to understand migration friction. Advocated for and helped shape the V1 chatbot feature based on what I heard directly from clients. 6-month migration completed - 100% of clients on new platform.

05 / Stats Assistant - The Feature That Changed Everything

A chatbot built for free that halved the support team.

This was originally a V2 feature. During the migration period, support calls spiked - directors new to the interface were asking a specific question repeatedly: "how many of my staff have completed the course?" and "who hasn't logged in yet?" These were questions the new UI answered visually, but new users weren't yet fluent.

After attending post-launch client meetings and hearing this directly, I made the case for pulling the chatbot into V1. We built a precision tool - not a general AI assistant, but a natural language interface that converted plain-English questions into database queries and returned direct, accurate answers with links to the relevant page.

It was built at zero additional cost - no AI subscriptions, no external APIs in V1. The existing database infrastructure was enough. The result was the biggest measurable business outcome of the entire project.

"How many staff have completed the courses?" -> Samantha Douglas - 1 course, Clara Tubiana - 1 course. Total: 2 staff completed all courses.

What this feature delivered

  • Support team: 7 -> 2. The single most significant confirmed business outcome of the project.
  • Built at zero additional cost - no AI subscriptions, no external APIs in V1. Existing database infrastructure.
  • Eliminated the most common support call type - "I can't find who completed what" - entirely.
  • Reduced migration friction at the highest-friction point - new users adapting to the new interface.
  • Validated the V2 AI roadmap - screenshot-based support and full conversational navigation now in planning.
06 / Key Design Decisions & Conscious Tradeoffs

What we chose, what we cut, and why.

Senior design is as much about what you don't build as what you do - and being able to articulate why. Every decision here had a real constraint and a documented rationale.

Chatbot pulled into V1 - originally planned for V2
Scope Decision · Real Story

This is the most consequential scope decision in the project. The chatbot was firmly in the V2 roadmap. During the migration period, support calls increased temporarily. After attending client meetings and hearing what was driving those calls - one specific question type about completion status - I brought that data back and advocated for an accelerated V1 version.

Why we accelerated it
  • Support calls concentrated on one query type: "who has/hasn't completed training?" - a database problem, not a UI problem
  • Existing DB infrastructure meant zero additional cost for V1
  • Migration period was the highest-friction moment - having the chatbot available then was far more valuable than six months later
  • Camp directors tested it during migration and responded positively immediately
The tradeoff accepted
  • V1 chatbot handles data queries only - cannot take actions or handle errors
  • Screenshot-based support (the original V2 vision) deferred - too expensive without AI subscription
  • Required re-prioritising approximately 2 weeks of development time
V2 roadmap confirmed

V2 planning is complete. Advanced AI features - screenshot-based error guidance, conversational navigation - are scoped and may require an AI subscription. The V1 chatbot proved the concept and demonstrated clear ROI before committing to that cost.
